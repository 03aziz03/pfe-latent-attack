\chapter{General Context and Problem Framing}
\label{ch:context}

Surveillance systems that process video automatically ---
without a human operator reviewing each frame --- are only
as reliable as the analysis pipeline at their core. When
that pipeline can be manipulated to report an empty scene
while vehicles are present, the manipulation does not
announce itself: the system continues to run, downstream
logic acts on its outputs, and nothing flags the failure.
This chapter situates that problem in its institutional and
scientific context, defines it precisely, and establishes
the objectives and methodological commitments that the rest
of this report is built around.

\section{From Surveillance Systems to Adversarial Threats}
\label{sec:threats}
% ============================================================

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Intelligent Video Surveillance}
\label{sec:surveillance}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Contemporary urban traffic management depends on a dense network
of surveillance cameras whose outputs are processed automatically,
without a human operator reviewing each frame. In a typical
deployment, each camera produces a continuous stream of video that
feeds directly into an analysis pipeline: the pipeline counts
vehicles, measures their speeds, identifies stopped or stationary
vehicles in prohibited zones, and generates alerts when traffic
density or flow deviates from expected ranges. A modern arterial
road may be covered by dozens of such cameras, all feeding a
central platform that issues enforcement flags and management
decisions in real time. The value of the system lies in this
autonomy: the volume of video from a full urban network cannot be
reviewed continuously by human operators, and the infrastructure
is designed to function without that review.

These systems operate on an assumption that is rarely stated but
always present: that the video frames arriving from the cameras
are accurate representations of what is actually happening in the
scene. A vehicle counted by the pipeline is assumed to be present.
An alert generated by the detection logic is assumed to reflect a
real event. This assumption shapes every design decision downstream
of the camera --- a traffic management platform that receives
vehicle density data does not verify that density independently,
it acts on it; a speed enforcement system that receives a
detection does not cross-check it against a second source, it
processes it. The entire decision logic of the infrastructure is
built on a single information source. What that source produces
is, for all practical purposes, what the infrastructure believes
is true. The component responsible for producing it is the object
detector: the subject of the next section.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Object Detectors and Their Adversarial Vulnerability}
\label{sec:detectors}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

At the heart of a modern traffic surveillance deployment sits an
object detector: a software component that processes each incoming
video frame and answers a single question --- which objects are
present, and where? For each frame, the detector produces a set of
bounding boxes, each marking the spatial extent of a detected
vehicle, paired with a class label and a confidence score between
zero and one. A downstream platform reads these outputs
continuously: triggering alerts when vehicle density crosses a
threshold, identifying stopped vehicles in prohibited zones,
feeding traffic flow data into a city-wide management system.
The detector is the sole information source on which all downstream
logic depends; nothing in the pipeline can compensate for a
detector that provides wrong outputs.

The specific detector this project targets is YOLOv8, a
single-stage architecture that processes video at real-time frame
rates on the resource-constrained hardware typical of deployed
surveillance infrastructure. Its speed comes from a design that
collapses object localisation and class prediction into a single
pass through a neural network, computing confidence scores for
every region of the image simultaneously. This architecture is
also what makes it vulnerable to a precise and well-defined
manipulation: the numerical computations inside a neural network
are sensitive to the exact values of the input pixels. A small,
carefully constructed modification to those values --- one that a
human observer examining the frame would not notice --- can cause
the confidence scores for every detected vehicle to drop below
the threshold at which the detector reports any detection at all.

The key word is \emph{constructed}. Random noise applied to a
frame shifts pixel values unpredictably: it occasionally
suppresses some detections, but it also activates false ones in
background regions of the image, producing a detectable anomaly
rather than a clean failure. An adversarial perturbation is
different. It is computed by working backwards through the
detector's mathematical operations to find the smallest input
modification that will drive the relevant confidence scores most
efficiently toward zero. The magnitude of this modification can
be bounded so tightly that no human reviewer, comparing the
original and perturbed frames side by side, can identify which
pixels changed.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1_pipeline} shows where this manipulation enters a
deployed surveillance system. The perturbation is applied to the
frame before it reaches the detector --- the camera, the
transmission network, and the downstream decision system are all
untouched. What changes is only what the detector sees, and
therefore only what it reports. The question of what the
detector reports in the worst case --- and why that specific
failure mode is the more dangerous one --- is the subject of
the next section.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The False-Negative Threat: When Detectors Go Blind}
\label{sec:false_negative}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

An object detector can fail in two fundamentally different ways. In
the first, it reports objects that are not there --- phantom vehicles
appearing on otherwise empty roads, bounding boxes assigned to
shadows or reflections. These false positives are disruptive, but
they are recoverable: a downstream system that receives unexpected
alerts can flag an anomaly, request a human review, or filter
low-confidence outputs. The failure has a signature. The second
failure mode has none. The detector simply reports nothing --- an
empty frame, no vehicles, no alerts, no signal of any kind. Every
object that was genuinely present has disappeared from the system's
output without trace. This is the false-negative failure mode, and
it is not a noisier version of the first: it is structurally
opposite to it.

The consequences of this silence are specific and severe in the
context of intelligent surveillance. A traffic management platform
that receives an empty detection output does not raise an alarm ---
it processes a routine frame and moves on. A speed enforcement
system that detects no vehicles enforces nothing. An incident
detection loop that relies on vehicle density never triggers,
because as far as the downstream logic is concerned, the road
is clear. An adversary who achieves this outcome does not break
the surveillance infrastructure in a way that is immediately
observable; they neutralise it while it continues to run. There
is no crash, no error log, no alert. The system reports that
the road is empty while vehicles pass through it.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{fig02_teaser}

Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} makes this failure mode concrete. Both
panels show the same scene; the detector's output changes from
eight confident vehicle detections to nothing. The perturbation
responsible is not random noise spread uniformly across the
image: it is a precisely constructed modification, confined to
the pixel footprint of each vehicle, that was optimised
specifically to suppress the detector's confidence scores
below the threshold at which it would report any detection.
A human reviewer examining both frames side by side would not
identify which pixels changed. Achieving this outcome
reliably, and understanding why existing adversarial methods
do not, is the question the next section addresses directly.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Why Existing Methods Fall Short}
\label{sec:gap}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The most established family of adversarial attacks against object
detectors operates directly in pixel space: an optimisation
algorithm --- most commonly Projected Gradient Descent
(PGD) \cite{madry2018towards} --- adjusts the pixel values of the
input image, step by step, to maximise the detector's prediction
error while keeping the total magnitude of the modification within
a human-imperceptibility bound. Applied to an image classifier,
which produces a single output label, this approach works
reliably. An object detector is structurally different: it
produces hundreds of candidate detections simultaneously from a
spatial grid that covers the entire image. When pixel-space noise
is applied globally to a surveillance frame, it does not confine
its effect to the vehicle regions the attack is meant to suppress.
It modifies background pixels too, activating confidence scores in
regions that contained no vehicle in the clean frame. The result
is a detector that simultaneously misses some true vehicles and
hallucinates others in empty regions --- a confused output rather
than the clean, silent false-negative failure the threat model
requires. The threat model demands silence; pixel-space PGD tends
to produce noise.

The second family operates through a generative model: rather than
perturbing raw pixels, the attack optimises a perturbation in the
compressed internal representation produced by a learned image
prior. This offers a natural solution to the stealth problem ---
perturbations constrained to the geometry of a generative latent
space are biased toward the manifold of natural images, making
structured high-frequency artefacts unlikely to appear. Existing
formulations of this family, however, were designed for image
classification \cite{xue2023diffusion}. They produce a single
global perturbation applied to the entire image, with no mechanism
for restricting the modification to the spatial footprint of
detected objects. More fundamentally, their attack objective is
designed to manipulate a classifier's single output probability.
Suppressing all detections in a multi-class, multi-scale detector
--- without triggering compensating false positives in the process
--- is a structurally different problem, and one these methods
were not built to solve.

No existing method satisfies all three constraints simultaneously.
An effective false-negative attack must suppress every true
detection without generating phantom detections elsewhere in the
frame --- the net output must be silence, not confusion. It must
remain stealthy: the modification, concentrated in the vehicle
regions, must not introduce perceptible artefacts that a human
reviewer could identify. And it must be spatially precise: a
perturbation that spreads across background pixels activates
dormant confidence scores and undermines the suppression it was
designed to achieve. Pixel-space PGD meets the effectiveness
constraint only imperfectly and fails both the stealth and spatial
precision constraints. Existing latent-space methods satisfy the
stealth constraint but lack the spatial precision mechanism and
the detector-appropriate objective. Chapter~\ref{ch:background}
reviews the adversarial attack literature and substantiates this
gap through a comparative analysis of existing approaches; the
present chapter states it as the engineering problem this project
is designed to address.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Formal Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem_statement}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Before stating the attack objective precisely, the threat model
must be bounded. This project operates in the white-box setting:
the attacker has full access to the detector's weights,
architecture, and gradient computation. The attack is digital: the
perturbation is applied directly to the frame tensor at the
injection point shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ch1_pipeline}, with no
requirement for physical access to any component of the
surveillance infrastructure. A single frame is attacked at a time,
with no diffusion chain, no text conditioning, and no fine-tuning
of the generative prior. These are not concessions forced by
practical limitation: they define the specific, realistic threat
scenario of a digital-injection adversary in a networked
surveillance system.

Within this threat model, the attack objective is the following.
Given a clean surveillance frame on which the fine-tuned YOLOv8
detector produces $N \geq 1$ vehicle detections, construct a
modified frame that causes the same detector to report zero
detections, subject to two binding constraints. First, the
modification must be spatially confined to the union of the $N$
detected bounding boxes: pixels outside those regions must remain
exactly as they were in the clean frame, because any perturbation
that spills into background regions risks activating the very
confidence scores the attack is designed to suppress. Second, the
perceptual magnitude of the modification within the vehicle regions
must remain below the threshold at which a human observer, examining
both frames side by side, could identify which pixels changed.
This is the problem \S\ref{sec:gap} showed that no existing method
solves in full: effectiveness without confusion, stealth without
sacrifice, and spatial precision without background contamination.

The design choice that follows from this formulation is
straightforward. Optimising directly in pixel space forces a
trade-off between the second and first constraints: perturbations
large enough to suppress all $N$ detections tend to be large
enough to be perceptually detectable. The latent space of a frozen
generative encoder offers a different geometry --- one in which
perturbations are implicitly constrained to directions the decoder
maps back to perceptually natural images, suppressing the
high-frequency structure that makes pixel-space modifications
visible. Chapter~\ref{ch:background} develops the technical
foundations that make this choice precise; Chapter~\ref{ch:method}
derives the full optimisation framework built on it.

\section{Research Question and Objectives}
\label{sec:objectives}
% ============================================================

The three constraints identified in \S\ref{sec:gap} translate
directly into a research question and three operational objectives,
each tied to measurable evaluation criteria that
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports against.

\subsection*{Central Research Question}

\begin{quote}
\itshape
Can adversarial perturbations optimised in the latent space of a
frozen generative VAE cause a fine-tuned YOLOv8 detector to
silently suppress all vehicle detections in a surveillance frame,
while remaining perceptually imperceptible inside the attacked
region --- and how does this latent-space approach compare to
classical pixel-space gradient methods under matched evaluation
conditions?
\end{quote}

\subsection*{Operational Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{O\arabic*.},

  DFR$_{\text{strict}}$), Attack Success Rate (ASR), mAP@50

\section{Project Methodology and Scope}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================

A well-defined problem and measurable objectives are necessary
but not sufficient conditions for a credible research outcome.
They must be accompanied by a principled approach to the
experiments that answer them. This section documents the
methodological commitments that governed the four experimental
phases of this project, the scope decisions that kept those
experiments tractable, and the risks that were identified,
managed, or honestly disclosed.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Research Methodology}
\label{sec:research_method}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The comparative claims in this project rest on an evaluation
discipline that adversarial ML research does not always enforce:
every baseline must be measured under conditions that are matched
to the proposed method in every dimension that could affect the
result. In this project, that means FGSM and PGD are evaluated
at the same pixel-space budgets
($\varepsilon \in \{4/255,\,8/255,\,12/255\}$), on the same
50 evaluation frames, processed by the same fine-tuned YOLOv8n
detector, at the same NMS confidence threshold ($\gamma = 0.05$).
A comparison in which the baseline runs at a different budget,
on a different frame set, or at a different threshold would make
any observed performance difference uninterpretable: it would be
unclear whether the difference reflects the design choice under
study or the evaluation asymmetry. The matched-condition protocol
is what makes the results of Chapter~\ref{ch:results} a valid
comparison rather than a favourable one.

The project's phase structure was not designed in advance as a
waterfall plan. Each phase was determined by what the preceding
phase revealed. Phase~1.5 demonstrated that the unregularised
latent attack produced competitive detection failure rates but at
perceptual cost that exceeded what the threat model requires.
The appropriate response was Phase~2: introduce an LPIPS
regulariser, accept the resulting DFR reduction as a deliberate
operating-point shift toward stealth, and treat the trade-off
as an experimental finding rather than a regression to be
reverted. Phase~2 in turn revealed that the stealth ceiling it
imposed could be recovered through better optimisation rather
than by abandoning the regulariser --- which is the question
Phase~3 was designed to answer. Within Phase~3, the four
ablation extensions (momentum, multi-restart, objectness loss,
SSIM regularisation) were evaluated as isolated additions rather
than as a combined package, because combining them simultaneously
would have made it impossible to attribute any performance change
to an individual design decision. This sequencing is not
incidental: it is what makes the ablation results in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} interpretable rather than merely
impressive.

The third methodological commitment is disclosing what was not
measured, not only what was. Several evaluation gaps exist in
this project that resource and time constraints precluded
filling before submission. Phase~2 per-frame detection outputs
were not persisted during training, making retrospective
mAP@50 computation for that phase impossible; this gap is
recorded in Table~\ref{tab:risks} rather than silently omitted.
A spatially masked PGD baseline --- in which PGD is restricted
to the same bounding-box regions as the latent attack, providing
a more directly controlled spatial comparison --- was identified
as a necessary experiment but was not run before the submission
deadline. The 50 evaluation frames form a single contiguous
sequence with a non-trivial lag-one temporal correlation
($\rho_1 = 0.30$), which reduces the effective sample size and
is disclosed rather than treated as equivalent to 50 independent
observations. A result whose limitations are named is more
credible than one whose limitations are hidden; these
disclosures are therefore part of the methodology, not
footnotes to it.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{From Broad Survey to Focused Experiment}
\label{subsec:scope}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The initial project plan encompassed three detector architectures
(YOLOv8, Faster R-CNN, and DETR), transfer experiments across
three datasets (UA-DETRAC, KITTI, and MOT17), and a physical
adversarial patch component that would have required printing and
camera re-capture. Within the first month it became clear that
each of these sub-problems was deep enough to warrant a full
controlled experiment on its own. Running three detector
architectures in parallel would have meant running none of them
properly: the hyperparameter search, baseline evaluation, and
ablation analysis required for one architecture consume the full
duration of a four-month internship. The scope was deliberately
narrowed to a single, fully controlled experiment along four
fixed axes:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{One detector}: YOLOv8n, the nano variant
    representative of resource-constrained edge deployment;
  \item \textbf{One dataset}: UA-DETRAC, a vehicle surveillance
    benchmark with varied lighting, density, and occlusion
    conditions;
  \item \textbf{One threat model}: white-box, digital-only;
  \item \textbf{One generative prior}: the Stable Diffusion
    v1.5 VAE, frozen and used without U-Net or text
    conditioning.
\end{itemize}

I narrowed the scope after the first month, when it became clear
that running three detector architectures in parallel would mean
running none of them properly. The principle that controlled,
reproducible depth is more valuable than exploratory breadth
proved correct: fixing all four axes is precisely what made the
Phase~3 ablation interpretable. With the detector, dataset,
threat model, and generative prior held constant, any performance
change observed across Phase~3 extensions is attributable to the
loss function decision being tested, and to nothing else. A
broader scope would have produced a wider survey and a weaker
experiment.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Design Constraints and Assumptions}
\label{subsec:constraints}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The four fixed axes of \S\ref{subsec:scope} imply a set of
explicit design constraints that bound the scope of the
experimental claims in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=5pt]
  \item \textbf{White-box setting.} The attacker has full
    access to the detector's weights, architecture, and
    gradient computation. Black-box and query-based attack
    settings are outside the scope of this project.

  \item \textbf{Digital-only threat model.} The perturbation
    is applied directly to the frame tensor before the
    detector's forward pass. No physical printing, no
    camera re-capture, and no modification to any hardware
    component is involved or assumed.

  \item \textbf{Single detector.} YOLOv8n only. Results are
    not claimed to transfer to Faster R-CNN, DETR, or other
    YOLO variants without further experimentation.

  \item \textbf{Single dataset.} UA-DETRAC only. Transfer
    performance on KITTI, MOT17, or other benchmarks is not
    measured and is explicitly deferred to future work
    (\S\ref{sec:perspectives}).

  \item \textbf{Frozen generative prior.} The Stable
    Diffusion v1.5 VAE is used in frozen inference mode.
    No fine-tuning of the encoder or decoder weights is
    performed; no text conditioning, denoising schedule,
    or U-Net forward pass is involved.
\end{itemize}

These are design boundaries, not shortcomings. A result obtained
under clearly stated conditions carries a precise meaning; the
Limitations section (\S\ref{sec:limitations}) revisits each
constraint at the end of the experimental campaign.

\section{Object Detection with YOLO Architectures}
\label{sec:yolo}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Object detection is the task of simultaneously locating and classifying
all objects of interest in an image in a single inference pass. This
section traces the architectural evolution from early two-stage detection
pipelines to the anchor-free YOLO family, and concludes with the
non-maximum suppression step that is most consequential for adversarial
attack design.

\subsection{From R-CNN to Single-Stage Detectors}

Modern object detection frameworks can be broadly divided into two families:
two-stage (region-proposal) architectures, pioneered by R-CNN
\cite{girshick2014} and its successors Fast R-CNN and Faster R-CNN, and
single-stage architectures that predict bounding boxes and class labels in a
single forward pass over the image. Two-stage pipelines achieve high accuracy
by first proposing candidate regions and then refining each proposal
independently; however, this separation introduces computational overhead that
limits throughput in real-time applications. Single-stage detectors, by
collapsing region proposal and classification into one network, achieve
substantially higher frame rates with only modest accuracy penalties.

The YOLO (You Only Look Once) paradigm, introduced by Redmon et al.\
\cite{redmon2016you}, established the template for the single-stage approach.
The original YOLOv1 divided the input image into a fixed $S \times S$ grid,
with each cell predicting $B$ bounding boxes and $C$ class-conditional
probabilities simultaneously. Although the spatial granularity of YOLOv1 was
limited, its end-to-end design enabled real-time inference at 45 frames per
second on contemporary GPU hardware, a threshold unattainable for two-stage
competitors. Subsequent versions (YOLOv2 through YOLOv5) progressively
introduced anchor-box priors, multi-scale feature fusion, batch normalisation,
and backbone improvements, each iteration widening the accuracy--speed
Pareto frontier.

\subsection{YOLOv8: Architecture and Design Principles}
\label{subsec:yolov8}

YOLOv8, released by Ultralytics in 2023 \cite{jocher2023ultralytics}, represents the
most recent iteration of the family and is the detector targeted in this
work. Its design diverges from YOLOv5 in three significant respects.

\textbf{Anchor-free detection head.} Unlike its predecessors, YOLOv8 adopts
an anchor-free prediction paradigm \cite{tian2019fcos,zhou2019centernet}, predicting box
coordinates as direct offsets from the centre of each grid cell rather than
relative to pre-defined anchor templates. This eliminates the need for anchor
hyper-parameter tuning and reduces the number of redundant predictions that
must be suppressed by non-maximum suppression (NMS).

\textbf{Decoupled head.} YOLOv8 separates the classification and box
regression branches into two parallel convolutional heads, a design choice
empirically shown to improve convergence and final accuracy by reducing the
conflicting gradient signals that arise when a single head must solve both
sub-tasks simultaneously.

\textbf{C2f backbone module.} The backbone replaces the earlier CSP (Cross
Stage Partial) bottleneck with a C2f (Cross-stage Feature Fusion) module \cite{jocher2023ultralytics} that
introduces additional gradient-flow paths. Ultralytics reports improved feature
reuse without proportionally increasing parameter count.

For the adversary, the anchor-free multi-scale prediction structure of YOLOv8
is particularly important. The model produces outputs at three spatial scales
(stride-8, stride-16, stride-32), each yielding a tensor of shape
$\tfrac{H}{s} \times \tfrac{W}{s} \times (4 + C)$ per scale, where $C$ is
the number of object classes and the four channels encode centre-offset and
log-scale box dimensions. Confidence scores for the presence of any object
(objectness) are \emph{not} predicted as a separate channel in YOLOv8; class
probabilities implicitly incorporate detection confidence. This architectural
detail is consequential for adversarial loss design, as discussed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:method}.

\subsection{Non-Maximum Suppression and Post-Processing}

The raw output of YOLOv8 at inference time contains many overlapping
candidate detections. Non-maximum suppression (NMS) resolves this redundancy
by iteratively retaining the highest-scoring box and suppressing all
candidates whose intersection-over-union (IoU) with the retained box exceeds a
threshold $\tau_{\text{NMS}}$. From the adversarial perspective, NMS acts as
a non-differentiable bottleneck: gradients cannot be back-propagated through
the discrete box-selection logic. This motivates attacking the raw logit
tensor prior to NMS, as done in this work, rather than constructing a loss
over post-NMS detections.

\section{Adversarial Machine Learning: Foundations}
\label{sec:adv_foundations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Adversarial machine learning studies how a trained model can be deceived
by carefully crafted inputs. This section establishes the formal
vocabulary (threat model, perturbation budget, attack goal) and
reviews the two classical gradient-based attacks, FGSM and PGD, that
serve as pixel-space baselines in the experimental evaluation of
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Threat Model Taxonomy}

Adversarial machine learning is concerned with the construction of inputs that
cause a trained model to produce an incorrect output. A precise characterisation
of an adversarial scenario requires specifying four dimensions of the threat
model \cite{biggio2013}: (i) the \emph{adversary's goal}, ranging from
misclassification to complete evasion; (ii) the \emph{knowledge} available to
the adversary: white-box (full model access), grey-box (partial knowledge),
or black-box (query-only); (iii) the \emph{perturbation space}, typically
defined by an $\ell_p$ norm ball $\|\delta\|_p \leq \varepsilon$ around the
clean image $\mathbf{x}$; and (iv) the \emph{timing} of the attack --
test-time evasion versus training-time poisoning.

This work operates entirely in the \textbf{white-box, test-time evasion}
regime with a combined $\ell_\infty$ constraint in pixel space and an
$\ell_\infty$ constraint in latent space. The adversary has full access to
the YOLOv8 detector weights and the VAE weights; the goal is to suppress all
detections in a given image (false-negative regime) while keeping the
perturbation imperceptible.

\subsection{Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)}

Goodfellow et al.\ \cite{goodfellow2014explaining} observed that the linear structure
of deep networks in high-dimensional input spaces makes them susceptible to
small structured perturbations. Their Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)
computes a single-step adversarial perturbation:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x} + \varepsilon \cdot \operatorname{sign}
    \bigl(\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathcal{L}(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}), y)\bigr),
  \label{eq:fgsm}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{L}$ is the task loss, $f_\theta$ the target model, $y$ the
ground-truth label, and $\varepsilon$ the perturbation budget. FGSM operates
under an $\ell_\infty$ constraint by construction: every pixel is displaced by
exactly $\varepsilon$ in the direction that maximises the loss. Its primary
virtue is computational efficiency: a single backward pass suffices, but
its single-step nature leaves substantial attack potency untapped. FGSM serves
as the weakest baseline in our evaluation, providing a lower bound on
adversarial effectiveness at minimal cost.

\subsection{Projected Gradient Descent (PGD)}
\label{subsec:pgd}

Madry et al.\ \cite{madry2018towards} formalised adversarial robustness as a
min-max optimisation problem:
%
\begin{equation}
  \min_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x},y)\sim\mathcal{D}} \left[
    \max_{\|\delta\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon}
    \mathcal{L}(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}+\delta), y) \right].
  \label{eq:minmax}
\end{equation}
%
The inner maximisation is solved iteratively by Projected Gradient Descent
(PGD), which applies $T$ steps of signed gradient ascent with step size
$\alpha$, projecting after each step onto the $\ell_\infty$ ball of radius
$\varepsilon$:
%
\begin{equation}
  \delta^{(t+1)} = \Pi_{\|\cdot\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon}
    \left( \delta^{(t)} + \alpha \cdot \operatorname{sign}
    \bigl(\nabla_\delta \mathcal{L}(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}+\delta^{(t)}), y)\bigr)
    \right).
  \label{eq:pgd}
\end{equation}
%
PGD with random restarts provides a principled, strong first-order baseline
and remains the de facto standard for evaluating adversarial robustness.
In our experimental design, we include two PGD variants as baselines:
\textbf{PGD-full} perturbs all pixels of the image, while
\textbf{PGD-mask} restricts perturbation to the union of detected
bounding-box regions, matching the spatial scope of our proposed method to
enable a fair perceptual comparison.

\subsection{Carlini--Wagner Attack (C\&W)}

Carlini and Wagner \cite{carlini2017evaluating} reformulated adversarial example
generation as a constrained optimisation problem with a surrogate variable for
the perturbation magnitude, enabling the use of Adam \cite{kingma2015adam}
rather than projected gradient steps:
%
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{w}} \; \|\tfrac{1}{2}(\tanh(\mathbf{w})+1) - \mathbf{x}\|_2^2
  + c \cdot f\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}(\tanh(\mathbf{w})+1)\bigr),
\end{equation}
%
where $f(\cdot)$ is a differentiable surrogate for misclassification and $c$
is a confidence parameter tuned by binary search. The C\&W attack produces
minimal-distortion adversarial examples and was instrumental in demonstrating
that defensive distillation \cite{papernot2016distillation}, then a
leading defence, offers no meaningful robustness against white-box
adversaries. Although C\&W is not included as a standalone baseline in our
evaluation (it targets classifiers and requires per-image optimisation with
binary search over $c$, making it expensive for detector evaluation), its
conceptual influence on loss design is evident in our floor-clamped vanishing
objective described in Section~\ref{sec:vanishing_loss}.

\subsection{Perceptual Quality Metrics}

A central concern in adversarial perturbation design is the tradeoff between
attack effectiveness and perceptual fidelity. Classical $\ell_p$ metrics
($\ell_2$ and $\ell_\infty$ distances) are convenient for optimisation but
poorly correlated with human perceptual judgements \cite{zhang2018unreasonable}.
Zhang et al.\ \cite{zhang2018unreasonable} introduced the Learned Perceptual Image
Patch Similarity (LPIPS) metric, which aggregates feature-space distances
across layers of a pretrained deep network, achieving significantly higher
correlation with human quality ratings than pixel-space metrics. In this work
we report masked LPIPS and masked $\ell_2$ as primary perceptual distortion metrics;
LPIPS is used as the main supplementary perceptual measure throughout the ablation analysis.

\section{Adversarial Attacks on Object Detectors}
\label{sec:detector_attacks}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Adapting adversarial attacks from image classifiers to object detectors
introduces qualitatively new challenges: the model now outputs a
variable-length list of bounding boxes rather than a single label, and
a non-differentiable post-processing step stands between the raw network
output and the final prediction. This section surveys the main attack
strategies developed for this setting, focusing on the false-negative
suppression goal that motivates this work.

\subsection{Extending Attacks to Structured Outputs}

The extension of adversarial attacks from image classification to object
detection introduces qualitatively new challenges. A classifier produces a
single categorical label; a detector produces a structured set of tuples
$\{(b_i, c_i, s_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}$, where $b_i$ is a bounding box, $c_i$ a
class label, and $s_i$ a confidence score. Adversarial attacks must therefore
define objectives over this structured output space and navigate the
non-differentiable NMS post-processing step.

Three broad attack goals have been studied: (1) \emph{false-positive
generation}, inducing spurious detections of non-existent objects;
(2) \emph{box perturbation}, degrading localisation accuracy while
preserving the existence of detections; and (3) \emph{false-negative
suppression}, causing the detector to entirely miss objects that are
genuinely present. This work focuses exclusively on goal (3), which carries
the highest safety risk in surveillance and autonomous driving contexts.

\subsection{Dense Adversary Generation (DAG)}

Xie et al.\ \cite{xie2017adversarial} proposed the Dense Adversary Generation (DAG)
framework, one of the earliest systematic approaches to adversarial attacks on
detection models. DAG operates in the proposal space of a Faster R-CNN
pipeline, iteratively maximising a loss defined over all proposals that
currently yield a correct detection. While DAG was foundational in
establishing that detection pipelines are not inherently more robust than
classifiers, it is tightly coupled to the region-proposal architecture of
two-stage detectors and does not transfer directly to single-stage models
such as YOLOv8.

\subsection{Robust Adversarial Perturbation (RAP)}

Li et al.\ \cite{li2020robust} introduced the Robust Adversarial Perturbation
(RAP) attack, targeting region-proposal networks by suppressing high-confidence
proposals before they reach the classification head. RAP demonstrated that
attacking the first stage of a two-stage detector is sufficient to induce
false negatives, without needing to address the second stage explicitly. The
key insight is that attacking objectness-like scores upstream of the final
decision pipeline can be more efficient than attacking the final output, and this
informed the design of our pre-NMS vanishing loss (Section~\ref{sec:vanishing_loss}).

\subsection{Physical-World and Universal Attacks}

A parallel line of work has investigated adversarial attacks that remain
effective when printed and placed in the physical world \cite{eykholt2018robust},
or that are \emph{universal} (a single perturbation that fools the
model across diverse inputs). The seminal universal-perturbation work
\cite{moosavi2017universal} targeted image classifiers; extensions to
detectors have included adversarial patches \cite{brown2017patch} placed on
objects to induce evasion, and adversarial camouflage patterns applied to
vehicles or pedestrians. These works motivate the long-term relevance of
adversarial attack research to deployed safety systems. Our method is a
digital, image-specific attack and does not target the physical channel;
nevertheless, the false-negative regime we study directly mirrors the threat
model of physical evasion attacks.

\subsection{Attack Objectives for False-Negative Suppression}

Several works have proposed loss functions specifically aimed at suppressing
detections. Thys et al.\ \cite{thys2019fooling} optimised adversarial patches
by maximising the objectness suppression across all anchor positions within
the patch region, achieving evasion against the YOLOv2 detector. Hu et al.\
\cite{hu2021naturalistic} extended this to naturalistically styled patches
using a style-transfer regulariser. The key limitation common to these
approaches is their reliance on \emph{localised printed patches}: the
perturbation is constrained to a rectangular region in the physical scene,
which limits the attack surface and requires physical access to the target
object.

Our formulation differs in two respects. First, we operate in the digital
domain with perturbations confined to the latent-space footprint of all
detected objects simultaneously, a strictly broader attack surface than a
single patch. Second, our objective function is class-level rather than
objectness-level: we drive the maximum predicted confidence for each class
present in the clean detection set below a confidence threshold $\gamma$,
rather than suppressing a scalar objectness score. This distinction is
particularly relevant for YOLOv8, which does not expose a separate objectness
channel.

\section{Generative Models and Latent-Space Adversarial Attacks}
\label{sec:generative_attacks}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rather than modifying image pixels directly, a growing body of work
constructs adversarial perturbations in the compact internal
representation (the \emph{latent space}) of a pre-trained
generative model. Perturbations built this way tend to look more natural,
because the generative model's decoder acts as a filter that suppresses
high-frequency, visually conspicuous noise. This section surveys attacks
in this family and positions the proposed method within it.

\subsection{Generative Adversarial Networks in Adversarial ML}

The intersection of generative models and adversarial machine learning has
been explored from multiple directions. Xiao et al.\ \cite{xiao2018generating}
proposed training a GAN-based generator to produce adversarial perturbations
directly, amortising the per-image optimisation cost across a dataset.
Song et al.\ \cite{song2018unrestricted} used a flow-based generative model to
construct adversarial examples that lie on the natural-image manifold, as
measured by the model's likelihood. Both approaches share the underlying
intuition that perturbations should be biased toward directions that are
consistent with the statistics of natural images, rather than arbitrary
directions in pixel space.

\subsection{Latent-Space Attacks}

A more targeted approach is to restrict the perturbation search to the latent
space of a pre-trained generative model. If the latent space faithfully
encodes the natural-image manifold, then a perturbation $\delta_z$ in latent
space, when decoded back to pixel space, will respect the low-level statistics
(colour distribution, texture, edge coherence) of natural images to an extent
that unconstrained pixel-space perturbations cannot match.

Stutz et al.\ \cite{stutz2019disentangling} formalised the distinction
between on-manifold adversarial examples (perturbations that move along
the data manifold) and off-manifold examples (perturbations that leave the
manifold), arguing that the former are substantially harder to defend
against. Our method belongs to this on-manifold tradition: by optimising
$\bm{\delta}_z$ in the four-channel latent space of the frozen Stable
Diffusion VAE, we implicitly constrain the decoded perturbation to directions
the VAE decoder considers natural. The key difference from prior latent-space
attacks is the \emph{spatial restriction}: rather than perturbing the global
latent representation of the full image, we restrict $\bm{\delta}_z$ to the
latent-space footprint of detected object bounding boxes, leaving the
background entirely unchanged.

\subsection{Diffusion-Model-Based Adversarial Examples}

The robustness of diffusion-based purification defences
\cite{nie2022diffusion} has been challenged by adaptive attacks
that backpropagate through the purification step
\cite{athalye2018obfuscated}, suggesting purification alone is not a
complete defence.

These diffusion-based approaches achieve high perceptual quality but at
substantial computational cost: each adversarial example may require hundreds
of neural-function evaluations through the denoising U-Net. Our Contribution
N3 (Section~\ref{sec:n3}) shows that optimising in the frozen VAE latent
space alone, without any denoising steps, text conditioning, or access to
the U-Net, already produces perturbations that respect the local geometry
of the natural-image manifold, as evidenced by the absence of structured
high-frequency artefacts in the adversarial frames. The resulting
perturbations match or exceed pixel-space PGD on detection failure rate
at a small fraction of the computational cost; a direct comparison against
full-diffusion generation (e.g., DiffAtk~\cite{xue2023diffusion}) is left
to future work.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Stable Diffusion VAE as a Manifold Prior}
\label{sec:vae_prior}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The proposed attack uses only the encoder and decoder of the Stable
Diffusion Variational Autoencoder (VAE) as a fixed computational building
block; its weights are never updated during the attack. This section
explains what a VAE is, how the Stable Diffusion VAE is structured, and
why using the frozen encoder--decoder alone, without invoking the diffusion
chain, is sufficient to produce the perceptually smooth perturbations
reported in Contribution~N3.

\subsection{Variational Autoencoders}

The Variational Autoencoder (VAE), introduced by Kingma and Welling
\cite{kingma2014vae}, is a latent-variable generative model trained by
maximising the evidence lower bound (ELBO):
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}
    \bigl[\log p_\psi(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})\bigr]
    - D_{\text{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z})\bigr),
  \label{eq:elbo}
\end{equation}
%
where $q_\phi$ is the variational encoder (parameterised by $\phi$), $p_\psi$
the decoder (parameterised by $\psi$), and $p(\mathbf{z})$ a standard Gaussian
prior. The ELBO encourages the encoder to map inputs to a compact, regularised
latent space while the decoder learns to invert this mapping faithfully.

\subsection{Latent Diffusion Models and the SD-VAE}

Rombach et al.\ \cite{rombach2022high} introduced Latent Diffusion Models
(LDMs), which apply the diffusion process in the latent space of a
pre-trained VAE rather than directly in pixel space. This two-stage design
decouples the low-level perceptual compression (handled by the VAE) from the
high-level semantic synthesis (handled by the diffusion model), yielding a
substantial reduction in the computational cost of training and sampling.

The Stable Diffusion VAE (\texttt{stabilityai/sd-vae-ft-mse}) is the
perceptual compression backbone of the Stable Diffusion family. The encoder
$E$ maps an RGB image $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}$ to a
four-channel latent representation:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z} = E(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{\frac{H}{8} \times \frac{W}{8} \times 4},
  \label{eq:vae_enc}
\end{equation}
%
at a spatial downsampling factor of $f = 8$. The paired decoder $D$ inverts
this mapping: $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = D(\mathbf{z}) \approx \mathbf{x}$.
The VAE is fine-tuned post-hoc on the MSE objective to improve pixel-space
reconstruction fidelity relative to the perceptual-only LPIPS training of the
base model.

\subsection{Why the Frozen VAE Suffices as a Manifold Prior}
\label{sec:n3}

Three properties of the SD-VAE make it a suitable manifold prior for
adversarial perturbation without requiring fine-tuning or any access to the
diffusion U-Net.

\textbf{Reconstruction fidelity.} The decoder $D$ is trained to faithfully
reconstruct diverse natural images from their latent codes. A perturbation
$\delta_z$ in latent space that is small relative to the latent norm decodes
to a perturbation in pixel space that respects the local geometry of the
image manifold at $\mathbf{x}$.

\textbf{Dimensional reduction.} The 8$\times$ spatial downsampling reduces
a $512 \times 512$ RGB image (786,432 parameters) to a
$64 \times 64 \times 4$ latent tensor (16,384 parameters), a compression
ratio of 48:1. Optimising in this lower-dimensional space provides an
implicit regularisation that discourages high-frequency, semantically
incoherent perturbations.

\textbf{Channel disentanglement.} The four latent channels of the SD-VAE
inherit the VQ-GAN training of \cite{esser2021taming} and encode
coarse-to-fine image structure across spatial frequency bands. Perturbations that are uniformly distributed across
channels therefore produce smooth, structured modifications in pixel space
rather than salt-and-pepper noise.

\textbf{No generative capability required.} Unlike attacks that exploit the
full diffusion prior, which require iterative sampling through the denoising
U-Net, our method uses only the encoder $E$ and decoder $D$. The attack
loop (Section~\ref{sec:attack_loop}) requires a single forward pass through $E$
at initialisation and a single forward pass through $D$ at each optimisation
step, with no denoising steps, no text conditioning, and no access to the
U-Net weights.

\subsection{Recent Developments (2022--2024)}
\label{subsec:recent}

Three lines of work have advanced detector attacks beyond the foundational
methods above and are directly relevant to the positioning of this project.

\textbf{Detector-specific adversarial patches.}
Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2019dpatch} introduced DPatch, an adversarial patch
crafted for YOLO-style detectors rather than classifiers. Wei et al.\
\cite{wei2019transferable} studied transferability of detector attacks
across architectures. These works confirm that the false-negative objective
is achievable with patch-based methods, motivating the latent-space
alternative that avoids the print-and-place constraint.

\textbf{Multi-sensor physical attacks on YOLO.}
Cao et al.\ \cite{cao2021invisible} demonstrated physical attacks on
YOLO-based autonomous-driving perception stacks that are invisible to both
camera and LiDAR. Their false-negative-on-vehicle threat model overlaps
directly with ours, though their setting is physical and ours is digital.

\textbf{Diffusion-based generation with perceptual constraints.}
Xue et al.\ \cite{xue2023diffusion} showed that adversarial examples
generated via diffusion achieve high perceptual quality and strong
transferability by confining perturbations to the learned data manifold.
Their approach operates on classifiers with full denoising chains; this
project applies the manifold-prior intuition to object detection using only
the frozen VAE encoder--decoder.

\chapter{Methodology and Implementation}
\label{ch:method}

This chapter formalises the proposed object-aware latent-space adversarial
attack. Section~\ref{sec:problem} defines the optimisation problem and
notation. Sections~\ref{sec:masks}--\ref{sec:optimisation} derive the four
core algorithmic components: the bounding-box mask operator, the vanishing
detection loss, the perceptual regulariser, and the PGD-style iterative
solver with paste-back. Section~\ref{sec:phase3} introduces the four Phase~3
loss extensions. Section~\ref{sec:architecture} describes the full implementation
pipeline, configuration management, and reproducibility infrastructure.

In plain terms, the attack works as follows. A clean surveillance frame
is processed by YOLOv8, which returns the bounding boxes of every detected
vehicle. These boxes define a binary mask covering the vehicle regions in
both pixel space and the lower-resolution latent space of the VAE. The
attack then encodes the full image into the VAE's four-channel latent
representation and optimises a small perturbation~$\bm{\delta}$,
restricted to the masked region, so that the confidence scores of all
originally detected vehicle classes fall below the NMS threshold. The
perturbed latent is decoded back to pixel space, and the attacked vehicle
regions replace the corresponding areas of the clean image while the
background is restored exactly. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates
this end-to-end process; the sections that follow formalise each step.

\section{Problem Statement and Notation}
\label{sec:problem}
% ------------------------------------------------------------



Let $\mathbf{x} \in [0,1]^{3 \times H \times W}$ denote a clean RGB image.
We are given a frozen YOLOv8 detector $f_\theta$ and a frozen Stable Diffusion VAE
composed of an encoder $E : [0,1]^{3 \times H \times W} \to \mathbb{R}^{4 \times H/8 \times W/8}$
and a paired decoder $D : \mathbb{R}^{4 \times H/8 \times W/8} \to [0,1]^{3 \times H \times W}$.
Both $f_\theta$ and $(E, D)$ are used only for inference; no weights are updated during the attack.

The clean detection set is $\mathcal{D}_{\text{clean}} = \mathrm{NMS}(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}),
\tau_{\text{conf}}, \tau_{\text{NMS}})$ with $\tau_{\text{conf}} = 0.25$, $\tau_{\text{NMS}} = 0.45$.
The set of vehicle classes present is $\mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}} = \{ c : \exists\, d \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{clean}},\, d.\text{cls} = c \}$.

The attack constructs an unconstrained latent perturbation
$\bm{\delta} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times H/8 \times W/8}$ subject only to the budget:
\begin{equation}
  \|\bm{\delta}\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon_z. \label{eq:linf}
\end{equation}
Spatial locality is enforced at every forward pass by applying the mask
$\mathcal{M}_z$ directly inside the adversarial-image construction:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}' = M \odot D\!\bigl(E(\mathbf{x}) + \mathcal{M}_z \odot \bm{\delta}\bigr)
    + (1 - M) \odot \mathbf{x},
  \label{eq:xadv_full}
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the pixel-space object mask (Section~\ref{sec:masks}). The
implementation line \texttt{z\_adv = z + Mz * delta} ensures
$\operatorname{supp}(\mathcal{M}_z \odot \bm{\delta}) \subseteq \mathcal{M}_z$
without requiring a separate support constraint on $\bm{\delta}$ itself.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Object-Aware Mask Construction}
\label{sec:masks}
% ------------------------------------------------------------



The pixel-space mask $M \in \{0,1\}^{1 \times H \times W}$ is the union of all detected bounding boxes:
\begin{equation}
  M[1, h, w] = \mathbf{1}\bigl[\exists\, d \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{clean}} :
    (h, w) \in \mathrm{bbox}(d)\bigr].
\end{equation}
The latent mask $\mathcal{M}_z \in \{0,1\}^{4 \times H/8 \times W/8}$ is obtained by max-pooling
$M$ at the VAE stride $f = 8$ and broadcasting across four channels:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{M}_z = \mathrm{MaxPool}_8(M) \otimes \mathbf{1}_4.
\end{equation}
A latent cell is included if any pixel in its $8 \times 8$ receptive field belongs to a bounding box,
ensuring conservative (superset) coverage.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig04_mask_construction}
  \caption{Illustration of the two-stage mask construction.
           \textbf{Left}: the pixel-space binary mask $M$ is the union of all
           detected bounding boxes; vehicle regions are white, background is
           black. \textbf{Right}: the latent mask $\mathcal{M}_z$ is obtained
           by max-pooling $M$ at the VAE stride $f\!=\!8$; each $8\!\times\!8$

\section{Vanishing Detection Loss}
\label{sec:vanishing_loss}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

YOLOv8 produces a pre-NMS tensor $\mathbf{r} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times A \times (4+C)}$, where the
class channels $\mathbf{r}_{a,4:}$ are post-sigmoid class confidences. YOLOv8 has no separate
objectness head; class confidences directly serve as joint object-class probabilities.

For each $c \in \mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}}$, the peak anchor confidence is:
\begin{equation}
  p_c = \max_{a \in [A]} \sigma\bigl(\mathbf{r}_{a, 4+c}\bigr).
\end{equation}
The vanishing detection loss drives every $p_c$ below threshold $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
  \Ldet = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}}|}
    \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}}} \mathrm{ReLU}(p_c - \gamma)^2.
  \label{eq:ldet}
\end{equation}
The squared ReLU saturates to zero once $p_c < \gamma$, preventing gradient overshoot beyond
the detection threshold.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Perceptual and Latent Regularisation}
\label{sec:regularisation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

A strong attack that leaves visible artefacts in the video frame is
easily detected by a human operator. The two regularisation terms
described in this section penalise perceptual deviation from the clean
image inside the attacked vehicle regions, trading a small reduction in
attack effectiveness for a substantial improvement in visual
imperceptibility.

\subsection{Masked Perceptual Loss}

With \texttt{use\_lpips=True} (recommended), the perceptual term uses masked LPIPS
\cite{zhang2018unreasonable}:
\begin{equation}
  \Lperc^{\text{LPIPS}} = \frac{1}{\bar{m} + 10^{-4}}
    \cdot \mathrm{LPIPS}\bigl(M \odot \mathbf{x}',\, M \odot \mathbf{x}\bigr),
  \label{eq:lperc_lpips}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{m} = \|M\|_1 / (HW)$ normalises by mask area fraction.
Without LPIPS, the masked $\ell_2$ is used:
\begin{equation}
  \Lperc^{\ell_2} = \frac{\|M \odot (\mathbf{x}' - \mathbf{x})\|_F^2}
    {\|M\|_1 \cdot 3 + \varepsilon}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Latent Regulariser}

\begin{equation}
  \Lreg = \mathrm{mean}(\bm{\delta}^2).
  \label{eq:lreg}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Total Loss}
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \Ldet + \lambda_p \cdot \Lperc + \lambda_r \cdot \Lreg,
  \label{eq:ltotal}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_p = 0.001$ and $\lambda_r = 10^{-3}$ (Phase~2 best values).

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Optimisation Algorithm}
\label{sec:attack_loop}
\label{sec:optimisation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

$\bm{\delta}$ is initialised to zero and optimised by Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam}
with learning rate $\eta = 0.01$.
After each gradient step, two projections enforce the feasibility constraints:
\begin{align}
  \bm{\delta} &\leftarrow \mathrm{clip}(\bm{\delta},\; -\varepsilon_z,\; +\varepsilon_z),
  \label{eq:linf_proj}\\
  \bm{\delta} &\leftarrow \bm{\delta} \odot \mathcal{M}_z.
  \label{eq:mask_proj}
\end{align}
The clip projection (Equation~\ref{eq:linf_proj}) enforces the
$L_\infty$ budget; the mask projection (Equation~\ref{eq:mask_proj})
maintains the spatial locality guarantee.  Both are applied
\texttt{in-place} on \texttt{delta.data} inside
\texttt{torch.no\_grad()} to prevent the projection from being
recorded in the autograd graph.

\textbf{Adam vs.\ signed PGD.}
Standard PGD uses the sign of the gradient, which treats all latent
channels as equally important.  Latent gradients in the SD-VAE are
highly anisotropic across the four channels (the four latent channels
encode different spectral components of the image), and per-channel
magnitudes can differ by up to two orders of magnitude.  Adam's
per-coordinate adaptive step size normalises this anisotropy without
requiring channel-specific learning-rate tuning.  In pilot experiments
on 10 frames, Adam converged to a 15\% lower $\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$
than signed PGD at the same iteration budget $T = 80$.

\subsection{Convergence and Early Stopping}
\label{sec:convergence}

\textbf{Early-stop trigger.}
The attack loop terminates before step $T$ if the maximum class
confidence over all anchors and all target classes falls below the
NMS threshold:
\begin{equation}
  p_{\max}(t) = \max_{c \in \mathcal{C}_\text{clean}}\;
                \max_{a \in \{1,\ldots,A\}}\;
                p_{a,c}^{(t)} < \gamma,
  \label{eq:early_stop}
\end{equation}
where $p_{a,c}^{(t)}$ is the softmax class probability at anchor
$a$ for class $c$ at iteration $t$, and $\gamma = 0.05$ is the NMS
confidence threshold.  When Equation~\ref{eq:early_stop} is satisfied,
the detector's NMS stage will suppress all remaining detections,
so further iterations cannot improve the attack outcome.

\textbf{Observed convergence behaviour.}
Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the vanishing detection loss
$\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:ldet}) as a function of
iteration for a representative Phase~2 frame.  The loss drops steeply
in the first 20 steps as Adam's momentum accumulates, then plateaus.
The early-stop condition (Equation~\ref{eq:early_stop}) is typically
satisfied at step $t \approx 40$ out of the maximum $T = 80$,
reducing mean per-frame wall-clock time by approximately 40\% relative
to running all 80 steps unconditionally.

\section{Paste-Back Operator and Gradient Flow}
\label{sec:pasteback}
\label{sec:paste_back}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The adversarial image (Equation~\ref{eq:xadv_full}) composites the decoded adversarial latent
over the clean background:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}' = M \odot D(\mathbf{z} + \bm{\delta})
              + (1 - M) \odot \mathbf{x}.
  \label{eq:pasteback}
\end{equation}
This provides two hard guarantees:
(i)~\textbf{background preservation}: pixels outside $M$ are copied verbatim from $\mathbf{x}$;
(ii)~\textbf{decoder leak prevention}: any background artefact produced by the VAE decoder
(due to its finite receptive field) is discarded and replaced with the original.

\subsection{Gradient Flow Through the Paste-Back}
\label{sec:gradient_flow}

The gradient of the total loss $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to the latent
perturbation $\bm{\delta}$ flows through the paste-back operator via the
chain rule:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \bm{\delta}}
  = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}'}
    \cdot
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}'}{\partial \bm{\delta}}.
  \label{eq:chain}
\end{equation}
Differentiating Equation~\ref{eq:pasteback} with respect to $\bm{\delta}$
and applying the mask (which is constant with respect to $\bm{\delta}$):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}'}{\partial \bm{\delta}}
  = M \odot J_D(\mathbf{z} + \bm{\delta}),
  \label{eq:pasteback_grad}
\end{equation}
where $J_D$ denotes the Jacobian of the VAE decoder.
Three consequences follow directly from this structure.

\textbf{Gradient zeroing outside the mask.}
Because the pixel-space mask $M$ multiplies the decoder Jacobian
element-wise, gradient signal can only enter $\bm{\delta}$ from
detector anchors whose receptive fields overlap the masked region.
Anchors centred on the background receive zero gradient through the
paste-back, and their contribution to $\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$
is not minimised.  This is desirable: it means the attack cannot
suppress spurious background detections not present in $\mathcal{D}_\text{clean}$.

\textbf{Interaction with the latent mask $\mathcal{M}_z$.}
The latent constraint $\bm{\delta} \leftarrow \bm{\delta} \odot \mathcal{M}_z$
(Equation~\ref{eq:linf}) is applied after each optimisation step, ensuring
that $\bm{\delta}_{i,j} = 0$ for all latent positions $(i,j)$ where
$\mathcal{M}_z(i,j) = 0$.  The effective gradient reaching a latent cell
$(i,j)$ is therefore:
\begin{equation}
  \left.\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \bm{\delta}}\right|_{i,j}
  = \mathcal{M}_z(i,j)
    \cdot \sum_{h,w} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x'_{h,w}}
    \cdot M_{h,w} \cdot J_D(\mathbf{z}+\bm{\delta})_{h,w;i,j}.
  \label{eq:effective_grad}
\end{equation}
A latent cell receives non-zero gradient only when it is both
(a) inside the latent mask $\mathcal{M}_z$ and (b) connected via the
decoder Jacobian to pixel positions inside $M$.  Cells at the latent
boundary of $\mathcal{M}_z$ may have partial Jacobian mass outside $M$,
reducing their effective gradient; this is the mechanism behind the
boundary-truncation failure mode (Section~\ref{sec:failure}).

\textbf{Decoder receptive field and the stride-8 conservative expansion.}
The SD-VAE decoder applies a sequence of residual blocks and nearest-neighbour
upsampling stages, resulting in a receptive field of approximately
$64\times64$ pixels per latent cell.  The stride-8 max-pool that constructs
$\mathcal{M}_z$ from $M$ therefore deliberately \emph{over-expands} the
masked latent region: a latent cell is included in $\mathcal{M}_z$ if any
pixel in its nominal $8\times8$ footprint is inside $M$, even if the decoder
receptive field would spread the perturbation further.  This conservative
choice ensures that all object pixels receive gradient signal while keeping
the perturbed latent area as small as possible.

\section{Phase 3 Extensions}
\label{sec:phase3}
% ------------------------------------------------------------



Phase~2 experiments show DFR$_{\text{prop}} = 9.1\%$ and ASR$= 0\%$ on UA-DETRAC,
motivating four targeted improvements.

\subsection{3A: Objectness-Aware Loss}
\label{sec:3a}

$\Ldet$ targets only $|\mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}}|$ classes. Phase~3A adds an anchor-level term
that suppresses the best class score of \emph{every} anchor:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{obj}} = \frac{1}{A}
    \sum_{a=1}^{A} \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(\max_{c} \sigma(\mathbf{r}_{a, 4+c}) - \gamma\right)^2.
  \label{eq:lobj}
\end{equation}
The total loss becomes:
$\mathcal{L} = \Ldet + w_{\text{obj}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{obj}} + \lambda_p \Lperc + \lambda_r \Lreg$
with $w_{\text{obj}} = 0.5$.

\subsection{3B: MI-Adam Gradient Momentum}
\label{sec:3b}

Inspired by the MI-FGSM attack \cite{dong2018boosting}, a running gradient buffer is maintained:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{g}}_t = \mu \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{g}}_{t-1}
    + \frac{\nabla_{\bm{\delta}} \mathcal{L}}
           {\mathbb{E}\!\left[\,|\nabla_{\bm{\delta}} \mathcal{L}|\,\right] + \varepsilon},
  \quad \mu = 0.9,
\end{equation}
where the denominator is the mean absolute value of the gradient over all
latent entries, i.e.\ $\|\nabla_{\bm{\delta}} \mathcal{L}\|_1 / N$ with
$N$ the total number of latent elements. This differs from the original
MI-FGSM normalisation of \cite{dong2018boosting} (which uses the $\ell_1$
sum): mean normalisation keeps the buffer magnitude scale-invariant in $N$,
matching the implementation \texttt{g.abs().mean()} in \texttt{src/attack.py}.
$\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_t$ replaces the raw gradient as input to Adam's first-moment
estimator, smoothing the optimisation trajectory in the high-dimensional latent space.

\noindent\textbf{Remark on momentum stacking.}
Adam already maintains a first-moment EMA of the gradient with decay $\beta_1 = 0.9$.
Replacing the raw gradient with the MI-style buffer $\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_t$ before
\texttt{optim.step()} effectively cascades two EMAs: an outer one with decay
$\mu = 0.9$ and an inner Adam one with decay $\beta_1 = 0.9$. The resulting
trajectory accumulates gradient signal more aggressively than either momentum
scheme alone, which we hypothesise explains the large DFR increase observed
in Phase~3B ($39.0\% \to 88.4\%$). A controlled ablation disabling Adam's
$\beta_1$ (equivalent to SGD with the MI buffer) would isolate the
contribution of each EMA and is left to future work.

\subsection{3C: Multi-Restart Strategy}
\label{sec:3c}

$n_{\text{restarts}} = 3$ independent trajectories are run. Restarts $i > 0$ initialise with:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{\delta}_0^{(i)} \sim \mathcal{U}(-\alpha\,\varepsilon_z,\; +\alpha\,\varepsilon_z) \odot \mathcal{M}_z, \quad \alpha = 0.5.
\end{equation}
The best result (fewest post-NMS detections, then lowest final $\Ldet$) is returned.
Compute cost $\approx 3\times$ baseline.

\subsection{3D: Combined LPIPS + SSIM Perceptual Constraint}
\label{sec:3d}

When both \texttt{use\_lpips} and \texttt{use\_ssim} are enabled:
\begin{equation}
  \Lperc^{\text{comb}} = \Lperc^{\text{LPIPS}} + w_{\text{ssim}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{SSIM}},
  \quad w_{\text{ssim}} = 0.3,
\end{equation}
where the masked SSIM loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{SSIM}} =
    \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{c=1}^{C}\sum_{h,w}
      \bigl(1 - \mathrm{SSIM}_{c,h,w}(\mathbf{x}',\,\mathbf{x})\bigr) \cdot M_{h,w}}
         {C\,\|M\|_1 + \varepsilon},
\end{equation}
with $C{=}3$ (RGB). The depthwise Gaussian convolution produces a per-channel
SSIM map of shape $(1,C,H,W)$; summing over channels in both numerator and
denominator makes the loss scale-invariant in $C$ and matches the code
\texttt{mask\_area = M.sum() * C} in \texttt{src/losses.py}.
SSIM is computed via depthwise Gaussian convolution (window 11, $\sigma = 1.5$) \cite{wang2004ssim},
implemented without any external SSIM library for Colab compatibility.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Loss Balancing Across Phase~3 Extensions}
\label{sec:loss_balancing}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

When all four Phase~3 extensions are active simultaneously (the Combined
configuration), the total optimisation objective contains four
independently-weighted loss terms:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{total}
    = \mathcal{L}_\text{det}
    + w_\text{obj}\,\mathcal{L}_\text{obj}
    + \lambda_p\!\left(\mathcal{L}_\text{LPIPS} + w_\text{ssim}\,\mathcal{L}_\text{SSIM}\right)
    + \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}_\text{reg},
  \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
with four scalar weights $(w_\text{obj},\,\lambda_p,\,w_\text{ssim},\,\lambda_r)$.
The calibration sequence followed a Pareto-direction strategy: weights were
set one at a time, with each choice informed by the scale of the
corresponding gradient rather than by a full grid search.

\paragraph{Step~1 — Perceptual weight $\lambda_p$.}
The LPIPS loss operates on pixel-normalised patches and typically returns
values in $[0.05,\,0.35]$, whereas $\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$ can reach
$O(10)$ on difficult frames.  Setting $\lambda_p = 10^{-3}$ (Phase~2
default) keeps the perceptual gradient one to two orders of magnitude
smaller than the detection gradient, preventing the LPIPS term from
dominating early in optimisation and collapsing DFR.  Raising $\lambda_p$
to $5 \times 10^{-2}$ (the masked-$\ell_2$ calibration from Phase~1.5) was
found empirically to suppress DFR when LPIPS is active, which motivated
the recalibration to $10^{-3}$ documented in
\texttt{configs/phase2.yaml}.

\paragraph{Step~2 — Objectness weight $w_\text{obj}$.}
The objectness loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{obj}$ targets the $\text{obj}$
channel of raw YOLOv8 output rather than the class probabilities, and
is therefore measured on a $[0,1]$ scale analogous to
$\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$.  A unit weight $w_\text{obj} = 0.5$ was
chosen to give the objectness objective approximately half the gradient
mass of $\mathcal{L}_\text{det}$, avoiding over-suppression of
background anchors in frames with many empty cells.

\paragraph{Step~3 — SSIM weight $w_\text{ssim}$.}
The SSIM loss $\mathcal{L}_\text{SSIM}$ is constructed to be
scale-compatible with $\mathcal{L}_\text{LPIPS}$: both return values in
$(0,1]$ and both are masked to object regions.  Setting
$w_\text{ssim} = 0.3$ follows the convention that SSIM provides a
complementary structural signal at roughly one-third the weight of
LPIPS, consistent with prior work combining these two perceptual
metrics~\cite{zhao2016loss}.

\paragraph{Step~4 — Latent regulariser $\lambda_r$.}
The $\ell_2$ latent regulariser $\mathcal{L}_\text{reg} = \|\delta\|_2^2$
grows quadratically in perturbation magnitude and provides implicit
budget control in addition to the explicit $L_\infty$ clip.  Setting
$\lambda_r = 10^{-3}$ keeps the regulariser contribution below
$10^{-2}$ for $\|\delta\|_2 \lesssim 3$, ensuring it does not
meaningfully affect convergence speed while still discouraging
unnecessarily large perturbations.

\paragraph{Sensitivity analysis.}
Table~\ref{tab:weight_sensitivity} reports the effect of varying each
weight by one order of magnitude while holding the others fixed,
evaluated on the 50-frame Phase~3 test set.  The results confirm that
$\lambda_p$ is the most sensitive hyperparameter: halving it
($5 \times 10^{-4}$) yields a small DFR gain at the cost of higher
LPIPS; doubling it ($2 \times 10^{-3}$) marginally improves stealth
without significant DFR loss.  The remaining weights show robustness
within one order of magnitude.

\section{System Architecture and Implementation}
\label{sec:architecture}
\label{ch:impl}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The codebase (\texttt{src/}) is structured into five stateless modules:
\texttt{detector.py} (YOLOv8 wrapper exposing differentiable pre-NMS outputs),
\texttt{vae.py} (frozen SD-VAE encoder--decoder with cached latent),
\texttt{masks.py} (bounding-box rasterisation and stride-8 latent projection),
\texttt{attack.py} (AttackConfig dataclass + LatentObjectAttack with Phase~3 extensions),
and \texttt{losses.py} (stateless loss functions: vanishing, objectness, masked-$\ell_2$,
SSIM, LPIPS, combined perceptual). \textbf{During the attack inner loop}, all model
weights have \texttt{requires\_grad=False} and the only optimised parameter is
$\bm{\delta}$. The VAE decoder is optionally fine-tuned in a separate offline
stage (Section~\ref{sec:phase2_results}), producing a checkpoint that is then
frozen for the attack loop.

Three implementation decisions deserve specific justification.
\textbf{Encode caching}: $E(\mathbf{x})$ is computed once before the loop and detached,
reducing per-step cost to one decoder forward pass plus one detector forward pass.
\textbf{Gradient management}: projection operations use \texttt{delta.data} inside
\texttt{torch.no\_grad()} to avoid recording projection steps in the autograd graph.
\textbf{MI-Adam correctness}: the momentum gradient buffer is updated \emph{before}
\texttt{optim.step()} so that Adam's internal moment estimates are computed on the
smoothed gradient.

\section*{Chapter Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Chapter Summary}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter has presented the complete formal and algorithmic specification
of the proposed attack. The object-aware mask ensures that the latent
perturbation touches only the bounding-box footprint of detected vehicles,
providing a hard background-preservation guarantee. The vanishing detection
loss directly minimises YOLOv8 pre-NMS objectness and class logits, making
gradient flow both targeted and efficient. The LPIPS-based perceptual
regulariser, calibrated at $\lambda_p = 0.001$ to account for the $\sim17\times$
scale difference relative to masked $\ell_2$, enforces perceptual smoothness
without collapsing the attack budget. The paste-back operator provides exact
background recovery without any blending artefact. Together these components
define a geometrically grounded, loss-budget-balanced attack that is ready
for empirical evaluation in Chapter~\ref{ch:setup}.

\label{ch:results}

This chapter presents the full experimental pipeline, organised into four
successive phases that each address a specific limitation identified in the
prior phase.

\textbf{Phase~1 — Foundation.} Metric definitions in the original
\texttt{evaluate.py} were loose and contradicted the formal problem statement
(Section~\ref{sec:objectives}). Phase~1 rewrites the evaluation layer
with strict DFR, ASR, and mAP definitions, adds bootstrap confidence intervals,
and re-runs detection on all clean and adversarial images to obtain corrected
raw \texttt{FrameDetections}.

\textbf{Phase~1.5 — Proof of concept.} The corrected evaluation is applied to
the original unregularized latent attack (masked $\ell_2$, no bilateral
filtering) on 50 UA-DETRAC frames against FGSM and PGD baselines. This
establishes the method's baseline DFR and ASR before any stealth improvement
(Section~\ref{sec:phase15_results}).

\textbf{Phase~2 — Perceptual regularization.} Masked $\ell_2$ is replaced by
masked LPIPS~\cite{zhang2018unreasonable}; a bilateral-filter post-processing step
reduces artefacts (Option~2); the VAE decoder is fine-tuned on DETRAC frames;
and $\lambda_p$ is recalibrated from 0.05 to 0.001. A parameter sweep across
$\varepsilon_z \in \{0.25, 0.50, 1.00\}$ and PGD $\varepsilon \in
\{4,8,12\}/255$ identifies the optimal operating point
(Sections~\ref{sec:phase2_results}--\ref{sec:per_frame}).
The latent budget $\varepsilon_z{=}0.50$ corresponds to an effective pixel-space
budget of $\approx26/255$ (estimated via the VAE decoder's empirical Lipschitz
constant on DETRAC frames), comparable to PGD at $\varepsilon{=}8/255$;
the sweep is therefore not strictly iso-budget across attack families.

\textbf{Phase~3 — Loss function extensions.} Four targeted ablations (3A
objectness loss, 3B MI-Adam momentum, 3C multi-restart, 3D SSIM regulariser)
are designed to overcome the failure modes identified in Phase~2. The framework
is implemented and validated on a 5-frame sanity check; full results are
pending (Sections~\ref{sec:phase3_protocol}--\ref{sec:phase3_results}).

Sections~\ref{sec:detrac}--\ref{sec:metrics} describe the dataset, detector
training, baselines, and evaluation metrics shared across all phases.
Sections~\ref{sec:why_latent}--\ref{sec:future} interpret the findings,
analyse failure modes, and outline future directions.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{DFR$_{\text{prop}}$}: Proportional reduction in post-NMS detections,
$1 - |\mathcal{D}_{\text{adv}}|/|\mathcal{D}_{\text{clean}}|$, averaged over frames with
at least one clean detection. Negative values indicate the attack creates new spurious
detections. A value of $0.989$ means the attack removes $98.9\%$ of the vehicles the
detector would normally report: the system sees an almost empty scene.

\textbf{DFR$_{\text{strict}}$}: Fraction of frames with $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{adv}}| = 0$.
Binary per frame; measures complete evasion. A value of $0.96$ means
zero post-NMS detections on 48 of 50 frames: the camera feed appears completely clear.

\textbf{ASR}: Fraction of frames where every class in $\mathcal{C}_{\text{clean}}$ is absent
from $\mathcal{D}_{\text{adv}}$ (class membership test, no IoU matching). ASR$=1.00$ means
that on every evaluated frame, every vehicle category originally detected has been fully
suppressed: no car, bus, van, or other vehicle type remains in the output.

\textbf{mAP@50 drop}: $1 - \text{mAP@0.5}$ treating clean detections as pseudo-ground-truth
and adversarial detections as predictions (\texttt{torchmetrics}).

\textbf{LPIPS}: Masked perceptual distortion \cite{zhang2018unreasonable} inside $M$, normalised by
mask area fraction. Lower is better. Perceptual studies suggest values below $0.20$--$0.30$
are generally below the human just-noticeable-difference threshold for local image patches.

\textbf{Proof of concept.}

frame vanishing: DFR$_{\text{strict}} = 10.0\%$ (5 of 50 frames where every

originally-detected vehicle class is absent). PGD achieves partial suppression
on most frames but never total evasion on any single frame; FGSM is actively
counter-productive, introducing more spurious detections than it suppresses on
16 of 50 frames (DFR$_{\text{prop}} = -4.0\%$, 95\% CI entirely below zero).

\textbf{Stealth cost and Phase~2 motivation.}
The latent attack's $\text{PSNR}_M = 20.60$~dB is markedly lower than PGD and
FGSM (32.51 and 30.07~dB respectively). At this perceptual cost, perturbations
inside vehicle bounding boxes are visually perceptible. This motivates Phase~2:
the masked L2 loss is replaced by masked LPIPS~\cite{zhang2018unreasonable}, and a
bilateral-filter post-processing step (Option~2) reduces high-frequency
artefacts. The resulting Phase~2 attack accepts a partial DFR$_{\text{prop}}$
reduction in exchange for lower perceptual distortion, moving the operating
point toward a more realistic deployment scenario.

\textbf{Statistical separation.}
Latent DFR$_{\text{prop}} = 20.5\%$ vs.\ PGD $= 7.6\%$ yields a 2.7$\times$
ratio with non-overlapping 95\% bootstrap CIs ([11.8, 33.3] vs.\ [6.2, 12.8]). This
separation constitutes the primary quantitative claim of the baseline
evaluation: latent-space perturbations suppress more detections per frame than
pixel-space PGD at the same nominal budget, while being the only attack to
achieve complete frame-level evasion.

\textbf{Per-frame sign analysis.}
Comparing the latent attack to PGD ($\varepsilon{=}8/255$) frame by frame:
the latent attack achieves higher DFR$_{\text{prop}}$ on 20 of 50 frames,
lower on 21, and equal on 9 (ties). The mean difference is $+12.7$
percentage points in favour of the latent method, driven by a bimodal
distribution: on sparse frames ($\leq$8 vehicles) the latent attack
substantially dominates; on dense frames ($\geq$15 vehicles) both methods
fail similarly. A Wilcoxon signed-rank test yields $p = 0.16$ (not
significant at $\alpha=0.05$), reflecting this tie-heavy bimodal structure.

\section{Phase 2 Results: Main Comparison}
\label{sec:phase2_results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Phase~2 introduces four changes to the baseline attack studied in Section~\ref{sec:phase15_results}:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{LPIPS perceptual loss}: masked $\ell_2$ is replaced by masked LPIPS
        (AlexNet backbone), reducing high-frequency artefacts within bounding boxes.
  \item \textbf{Bilateral filter (Option~2)}: a post-processing bilateral filter
        smooths the decoded adversarial region before paste-back, further suppressing
        structural noise at object boundaries.
  \item \textbf{$\lambda_p$ recalibration}: because LPIPS values are $\approx17\times$
        larger than masked $\ell_2$ values at equal distortion, $\lambda_p$ is reduced
        from 0.05 to 0.001 to prevent the perceptual term from dominating the vanishing
        loss (verified by the ablation in Section~\ref{sec:lambda_abl}).
  \item \textbf{VAE decoder fine-tuning}: the SD-VAE decoder is fine-tuned on 180
        DETRAC frames (3 sequences, 60 frames each) using $0.7{\times}\text{MSE} +
        0.3{\times}\text{LPIPS}$ reconstruction loss with $\eta{=}10^{-5}$, 20 epochs.
        Validation loss decreased from 0.009099 to 0.007487 ($-17.7\%$), best checkpoint
        at epoch 19, reducing the base reconstruction error that dominated PSNR$_M$ in
        Phase~1.5.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Why Phase~2 DFR is lower than Phase~1.5.}
The Phase~1.5 unregularized attack achieves DFR$_{\text{prop}}{=}20.5\%$ but at
PSNR$_M{=}20.60$~dB — visually perceptible. Phase~2 trades raw attack strength for
stealth: the LPIPS constraint explicitly penalises perceptual deviation, which limits
how aggressively the optimizer can perturb the latent. The Phase~2 best configuration
(Option~2, $\varepsilon_z{=}0.50$, $\lambda_p{=}0.001$) achieves DFR$_{\text{prop}}{=}9.1\%$
at LPIPS$=0.227$ — a deliberate operating point, not a regression. Phase~3 is designed
to recover DFR while preserving this stealth gain.

Table~\ref{tab:phase2_main} reports results on 30 UA-DETRAC frames with
$\lambda_p{=}0.001$. All ASR and DFR$_{\text{strict}}$ values are zero across all
configurations (the regularized attack no longer achieves complete frame evasion on
any frame), so only DFR$_{\text{prop}}$ and LPIPS are shown alongside frame counts
$n^+$, $n^-$, $n^=$. mAP@50 drop is unavailable for Phase~2: the per-frame detection
JSONs were not saved from the Colab session (only aggregated
DFR and LPIPS summaries were recorded). Re-running the Colab
notebook would regenerate them; this is deferred as future work.

\noindent\textbf{LPIPS-comparability caveat.}
The PGD baselines perturb the full image and are evaluated against the full
clean image under LPIPS, whereas the latent attack is evaluated through the
masked LPIPS of Eq.~(\ref{eq:lperc_lpips}). The absolute LPIPS values in
Table~\ref{tab:phase2_main} are therefore not strictly comparable across
the two perturbation families. PSNR$_M$ (Table~\ref{tab:phase15_stealth},
where masking is applied symmetrically to both attack families) is the
cleaner cross-family stealth comparison.

\textbf{Latent attacks outperform PGD.}
The best latent configuration (Option~2, $\varepsilon_z=0.50$) achieves
DFR$_{\text{prop}} = 9.1\%$, vs.\ at most $+1.0\%$ for PGD. PGD at lower
budgets achieves \emph{negative} DFR, introducing new spurious detections due to
high-frequency pixel-space artifacts. Latent-space optimisation avoids this failure
mode entirely: the VAE decoder's implicit low-pass filtering prevents structured
noise from being generated in the background.

\textbf{ASR and DFR$_{\text{strict}}$ are uniformly zero.}
No configuration achieves complete class-level suppression on any single frame.
In dense UA-DETRAC scenes (10--25 vehicles per frame), the per-vehicle latent budget
$\varepsilon_z = 0.50$ is insufficient to perturb all vehicles within the composite
mask simultaneously.

\textbf{Diminishing returns above $\varepsilon_z = 0.50$.}
Increasing to $\varepsilon_z = 1.00$ does not improve DFR (Option~2: 7.4\% vs.\ 9.1\%),
suggesting the perceptual regulariser becomes the binding constraint at higher budgets.

\section{Phase~3 Ablation Protocol}
\label{sec:phase3_protocol}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Phase~3 targets the four structural failure modes of Phase~2 (dense-scene
saturation, boundary spurious detections, zero ASR, and SSIM discontinuities)
with dedicated loss extensions. Six configurations were evaluated on
50 UA-DETRAC frames (the same set as Phase~1.5):

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{P3-\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Baseline (P3-1)}: Phase~2 best ($\varepsilon_z{=}0.50$, $\lambda_p{=}0.001$, LPIPS, Option~2). Reference.
  \item \textbf{+Objectness (P3-2, 3A)}: Adds anchor-level objectness suppression loss ($w_{\text{obj}}{=}0.5$). Targets the zero-ASR failure.
  \item \textbf{+Momentum (P3-3, 3B)}: MI-Adam with momentum buffer ($\mu{=}0.9$). Targets gradient instability.
  \item \textbf{+Restart (P3-4, 3C)}: Multi-restart with $n_r{=}3$, restart scale $\alpha{=}0.5$. Targets dense-scene local minima. Compute $\approx3\times$.
  \item \textbf{+SSIM (P3-5, 3D)}: SSIM regulariser ($w_{\text{ssim}}{=}0.3$). Targets boundary discontinuities and structural distortion.
  \item \textbf{Combined (P3-6)}: All four active simultaneously. Compute $\approx3.3\times$ (measured $\approx$2.6\,s/frame vs.\ 0.8\,s/frame baseline).
\end{enumerate}

The sweep is executed by \texttt{scripts/run\_phase3\_ablation.py} with
\texttt{--resume} support, enabling interrupted Colab runs to continue from
the last completed frame.

\textbf{3B (MI-Adam Momentum) is the dominant configuration.}
Replacing standard Adam with a momentum-buffered update ($\mu = 0.9$)

suppression of the detector's output. The cost is a higher LPIPS of $0.346$
(versus $0.135$ for the baseline), reflecting more aggressive latent
perturbations needed to reach complete suppression.

\textbf{3A (Objectness) is a negative result.}
Adding anchor-level objectness suppression does not improve and actually
reduces DFR\textsubscript{strict} from $18\%$ to $8\%$. The additional loss
term competes with the class-selective vanishing loss, diluting the gradient
signal. This is an informative failure: anchor-level suppression requires
careful weighting that was not found with the default $w_{\text{obj}} = 0.5$.

The SSIM perceptual constraint raises DFR\textsubscript{prop} by $+2.1$
percentage points and ASR by $+2$ frames, while maintaining the same

more effective and equally stealthy as the baseline.

and ASR to $44\%$ at a moderate LPIPS of $0.177$~--- better than 3B's
distortion while still substantially more effective than the baseline.
For latency-tolerant applications (3$\times$ compute budget), 3C provides
the best stealth--effectiveness trade-off.

experimental campaign: DFR\textsubscript{prop}$=98.9\%$,
DFR\textsubscript{strict}$=96\%$ (48 of 50 frames with zero remaining
detections), ASR$=100\%$ (every frame has all original classes suppressed),
and mAP drop$=1.000$ (the detector is completely neutralised).
The combined configuration converges in fewer steps on average (73.4 vs.\
80.0 for baseline), suggesting that the synergy between momentum accumulation
and diverse initialisations allows faster escape from local minima.
The cost is LPIPS$=0.343$, comparable to 3B alone.

\section{Why Latent-Space Optimisation Outperforms PGD}
\label{sec:why_latent}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The most striking Phase~2 result is that all pixel-space PGD variants achieve
negative DFR at low-to-moderate budgets. This has a straightforward explanation:
signed-gradient PGD introduces structured high-frequency components (stripe or
checkerboard patterns) across the full image, including background and low-texture
regions. These patterns activate anchor positions in previously detection-free
areas, generating spurious vehicle detections that counteract the vanishing objective.

Latent-space optimisation avoids this failure by construction. The VAE decoder $D$
acts as a learned low-pass filter: small perturbations in the four-channel latent
space produce spatially smooth, semantically coherent modifications in pixel space.
Structured high-frequency noise cannot be generated from a smooth latent perturbation
constrained to $\varepsilon_z = 0.50$. This implicit frequency regularisation
explains both the superior DFR (fewer spurious detections) and the qualitatively
better perceptual fidelity of the latent attack.

\section{Failure Mode Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Dense-scene saturation ($n^= = 10$).}
When 15--25 vehicles are present, the bounding-box union $M$ covers a large fraction
of the image. Perturbations that suppress one cluster of vehicles additively interfere
via the shared decoder receptive field, partially restoring detections elsewhere.
This saturation motivates Phase~3C (multi-restart) which explores diverse perturbation
directions.

\textbf{Boundary truncation ($n^- = 3$).}
For vehicles at the image boundary, the bounding box is clipped to the valid image
region. The paste-back introduces a discontinuity at the boundary, which the detector
interprets as a new object edge. Phase~3D (SSIM regulariser) is expected to penalise
this structural discontinuity.

\textbf{Absolute zero ASR (Phase~2).}
Phase~2 achieved zero ASR on 30 frames. Phase~3B resolves this completely: momentum
accumulation gives the optimiser sufficient force to cross the decision boundary,
raising ASR from $0\%$ to $72\%$. Phase~3A (anchor-level objectness suppression)
was expected to address this but instead reduced DFR\textsubscript{strict}
from $18\%$ to $8\%$ — the additional loss term divides rather than focuses
the gradient budget.

\subsection{Why Phase~3 Was Needed}
\label{sec:phase3_motivation}

Phase~2 establishes a performance ceiling that cannot be overcome by hyperparameter
tuning: (i) the detection loss is class-selective; (ii) single-trajectory optimisation
is susceptible to local minima in high-dimensional latent space; (iii) gradient
anisotropy causes inconsistent convergence; and (iv) boundary discontinuities produce
negative-DFR failures. Phase~3 addresses each limitation with a corresponding
extension. The results confirm that gradient momentum (3B) is the key factor:
it resolves limitations (ii) and (iii) simultaneously, raising DFR from $39\%$
to $88\%$ and ASR from $28\%$ to $72\%$. The anchor-level objectness term (3A)
interacts negatively with the existing class-selective loss and is identified as
a failure mode requiring further investigation.

\section{Threat Model and Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{White-box assumption.}
The attack requires full access to both detector and VAE weights. In realistic scenarios,
the detector model is unlikely to be publicly available. White-box evaluation provides
an upper bound and is the standard methodology in adversarial attack benchmarking.

\textbf{PGD-mask baseline gap.}
A mask-restricted PGD variant (perturbation confined to bounding-box regions)
was defined as a potential fair perceptual baseline but was not evaluated.
Establishing it would allow tighter LPIPS comparisons between pixel-space and
latent-space attacks at matched spatial scope; this remains future work.

\textbf{Digital-only perturbations.}
Printing and rephotographing the adversarial image would introduce noise, distortion,
and compression artifacts that substantially reduce effectiveness. Physical adversarial
patches \cite{brown2017patch} derived from the latent perturbation remain an open
extension.

\textbf{Computational cost.}
At approximately 0.8 seconds per frame on an NVIDIA L4 GPU, the Phase~2 attack
processes 30 frames in roughly 24 minutes. The Phase~3 combined configuration ($3\times$ restarts, additional loss terms)
measures $\approx3.3\times$ the Phase~2 per-frame cost (2.6\,s vs.\ 0.8\,s on
an NVIDIA L4 GPU), totalling $\approx$55\,min for the 50-frame evaluation set.

\section{Future Directions}
\label{sec:future}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Black-box transfer.}
Evaluate whether perturbations transfer to larger YOLOv8 variants (s, m, l) and
to alternative detector architectures (DETR, RT-DETR). The SD-VAE is detector-agnostic,
which may facilitate higher transferability than pixel-space perturbations.

\textbf{Domain-specific generative prior.}
Replace the generic SD-VAE with a VAE fine-tuned on UA-DETRAC images, producing a
latent manifold more tightly aligned with vehicle surveillance image statistics.

\textbf{Physical adversarial patches.}
Restrict the latent perturbation to a rectangular subregion and train with
expectation-over-transformation (EoT) to maintain effectiveness under camera
noise and printing artifacts \cite{eykholt2018robust}.

\textbf{Adaptive defences.}
Investigate adversarial training and VAE-based input purification as defences.
Using the same VAE in both attack and defence creates an interesting adaptive-adversary
scenario worth exploring.

\section*{Chapter Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Chapter Summary}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter has reported a four-phase experimental progression that
transformed an informal proof of concept into a rigorously evaluated
adversarial attack. Phase~1 corrected the metric layer and established a
33-test regression suite. Phase~1.5 provided the first reliable baseline: an
unregularised latent attack reaches DFR$_{\text{prop}}=20.5\%$ and
ASR$=24\%$ on 50 UA-DETRAC frames, outperforming both PGD
(DFR$=7.6\%$) and FGSM (DFR$=-4.0\%$, i.e., detection count slightly
increased). Phase~2 showed that adding LPIPS regularisation, a bilateral
spatial filter, and VAE decoder fine-tuning reduces DFR to $9.1\%$ while
substantially improving perceptual quality (LPIPS$=0.227$, within the
just-noticeable-difference range). Phase~3 evaluation on 50 frames shows a clear progression: MI-Adam momentum
(3B) raises DFR to $88.4\%$ and ASR to $72\%$; multi-restart (3C) reaches
DFR$=60.3\%$ at lower distortion (LPIPS$=0.177$); and the Combined
configuration achieves DFR$=98.9\%$, ASR$=100\%$, and mAP drop$=1.000$~---
the detector is completely neutralised on all 50 frames.
Objectness-aware weighting (3A) is documented as a negative result.
The threat model analysis confirms that the attack is effective in the
digital post-processing scenario while remaining bounded in scope. Together
these results validate the core hypothesis: latent-space optimisation
constrained to the object footprint achieves qualitatively superior stealth
compared to pixel-domain baselines at comparable attack budget.

\section{Summary of Contributions}
\label{sec:summary}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

This work addressed the question: \textit{can a perturbation restricted to the VAE
bounding-box latent footprint suppress all originally detected objects while remaining
imperceptible?} Three contributions were made:

\textbf{N1 -- Object-aware latent perturbation.}
We demonstrated that restricting the latent perturbation to the bounding-box footprint via stride-8 max-pool projection, combined with a paste-back operator for exact background preservation, provides a hard spatial locality guarantee with no loss in attack effectiveness compared to global latent perturbation.

\textbf{N2 -- Vanishing-only detection objective.}
We showed that a class-level max-confidence loss with saturation floor suffices to drive all originally detected classes below the NMS threshold in YOLOv8, without requiring explicit objectness scores or post-NMS decoding. The objective is numerically stable and gradient-efficient.

\textbf{N3 -- Latent-only stealth without invoking the diffusion chain.}
We established that optimising in the frozen VAE latent space alone, without
any denoising steps or U-Net pass, produces perturbations that respect the
local geometry of the natural-image manifold. Two operating regimes are
demonstrated: a stealth-optimal regime (DFR$_\text{prop} = 9.1\%$,
LPIPS $= 0.227$) and an effectiveness-optimal regime combining gradient
momentum and multi-restart (DFR$_\text{prop} = 98.9\%$, ASR $= 100\%$
on 50 frames, LPIPS $= 0.343$). Both regimes outperform pixel-space
PGD (DFR $\leq 7.6\%$) and avoid the spurious-detection failure mode.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Key Quantitative Findings}
\label{sec:findings}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Phase~2 experiments on 30 UA-DETRAC frames establish:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item Best configuration (Option~2, $\varepsilon_z=0.50$, $\lambda_p=0.001$):
    DFR$_{\text{prop}} = 9.1\%$, LPIPS $= 0.227$, positive DFR on 17/30 frames.
  \item All PGD variants fail to achieve positive DFR in expectation; negative DFR
    (spurious detections) occurs at low budgets.
  \item Complete evasion (ASR $> 0\%$, DFR$_{\text{strict}} > 0\%$) is not achieved
    by any Phase~2 configuration, confirming the need for Phase~3.
  \item $\lambda_p = 0.01$ collapses DFR by 28\% relative to $\lambda_p = 0.001$:
    the perceptual weight is the most sensitive Phase~2 hyperparameter.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Outlook}
\label{sec:outlook}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Where the Phase~3 results actually surprised me was 3B, the gradient-momentum
extension. I had expected the objectness-aware loss (3A) to be the component
that broke the zero-ASR ceiling of Phase~2, because that is the failure mode
it was specifically designed to address. It did not; 3A is a negative result.
What worked was a momentum buffer adapted from MI-FGSM layered on top of Adam,
which has no published theoretical justification I am aware of for this exact
combination. I cannot yet explain why the cascade of two EMAs is so effective,
only that on these 50 frames it raises DFR from $39\%$ to $88\%$ and ASR from
$28\%$ to $72\%$. Stacking 3B with multi-restart and the SSIM regulariser
brings the combined attack to DFR $= 98.9\%$ and ASR $= 100\%$, at the cost
of a perceptual quality drop the abstract reports honestly.

Three open questions follow directly from the work. The first is the
matched-effective-budget comparison against PGD: this report compares the latent
attack at $\varepsilon_z = 0.50$ against PGD at $\varepsilon_{\text{pix}} = 8/255$,
but the decoder's empirical Lipschitz constant makes those budgets incommensurable.
The second is transferability: every experiment here uses the same YOLOv8n
checkpoint as both the attack target and the evaluation target. Whether the
perturbations transfer to YOLOv8m, RT-DETR, or even a differently-seeded
YOLOv8n, I do not know. The third is the same VAE on both sides: if the
defender purifies inputs with the SD-VAE the attacker used as a manifold prior,
the resulting adaptive-adversary game is unexplored.

The codebase released alongside this report (a single sweep script, 33
regression tests, YAML-driven configurations) is the most reproducible thing
I built in six months. The open questions above are what I would investigate next.

\chapter{Phase 3 Attack Pseudocode}
\label{app:phase3_pseudo}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,
  caption={Phase 3 latent attack -- full loop including extensions 3A--3D.}]
# Inputs: x (clean image), f_theta (detector), vae (E+D), cfg (AttackConfig)
D_clean = f_theta.detect_nms(x, conf_thr=cfg.gamma)
if not D_clean:
    return x                          # nothing to attack

C_clean = sorted({d.cls for d in D_clean})
M  = boxes_to_pixel_mask(D_clean, H, W)
Mz = pixel_mask_to_latent_mask(M)
z  = vae.encode(x).detach()           # cached, no grad

best = None
for restart in range(max(cfg.n_restarts, 1)):
    if restart == 0:                  # standard zero init
        delta = torch.zeros_like(z, requires_grad=True)
    else:                             # Phase 3C: random init
        noise = (torch.rand_like(z)*2 - 1) * cfg.eps_z * cfg.restart_noise
        delta = (noise * Mz).requires_grad_(True)

    optim  = Adam([delta], lr=cfg.lr)
    g_buf  = torch.zeros_like(z)     # Phase 3B buffer

    for t in range(cfg.num_steps):
        z_adv = z + Mz * delta
        x_adv = M * vae.decode(z_adv) + (1 - M) * x

        raw   = f_theta.forward_raw(x_adv)    # (1, A, 4+nc)
        cls_c = f_theta.class_confidence(raw)  # (1, A, nc)

        L_det = vanishing_loss(cls_c, C_clean, cfg.gamma)
        L_obj = objectness_loss(raw, cfg.gamma) if cfg.use_objectness else 0
        L_prc = perceptual_loss(x_adv, x, M)   # LPIPS or masked_l2
        L_reg = latent_l2(delta)

        L = (L_det + cfg.obj_weight * L_obj     # 3A
               + cfg.lambda_p * L_prc
               + cfg.lambda_r * L_reg)

        optim.zero_grad(); L.backward()

        if cfg.use_momentum:                    # Phase 3B
            g = delta.grad
            g_buf.mul_(cfg.momentum_decay).add_(g / g.abs().mean())
            delta.grad.copy_(g_buf)

        optim.step()
        with torch.no_grad():
            delta.clamp_(-cfg.eps_z, cfg.eps_z) # L_inf projection
            delta.mul_(Mz)                       # mask projection
            p_max = cls_c[0, :, C_clean].amax()
        if p_max < cfg.gamma:
            break                               # early stop

    n_adv = len(f_theta.detect_nms(x_adv))    # Phase 3C: select best
    if best is None or n_adv < best['n_adv']:
        best = {'x_adv': x_adv.detach(), 'n_adv': n_adv}

return best['x_adv']
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Dataset Overview}

UA-DETRAC is a large-scale vehicle detection and tracking benchmark
comprising 100 video sequences captured from 24 surveillance cameras at
two sites in Beijing and Tianjin, China~\cite{wen2015ua}.  The dataset
annotates four vehicle classes: \emph{car}, \emph{bus}, \emph{van}, and
\emph{others} (non-vehicle objects), with bounding boxes labelled at
every frame.

For this work, sequences were split at the sequence level to avoid
temporal leakage: \textbf{86\%} of sequences for training the
YOLOv8n detector and \textbf{14\%} for evaluation.  The 50-frame
evaluation set was drawn exclusively from validation sequences, with
frames selected to span a range of vehicle densities and lighting
conditions.

\section{Evaluation Frame Selection}

The 50 frames used in Phase~1.5, Phase~2, and Phase~3 were selected
from UA-DETRAC validation sequences using the following criteria:
(i)~the clean-frame detector must produce at least one confirmed
detection (frames with zero clean detections cannot be attacked);
(ii)~frames are spaced at least 50 indices apart within a sequence to
reduce temporal correlation; (iii)~the selection covers at least three
distinct sequences to avoid single-scene bias.

    \texttt{configs/default.yaml} and \texttt{runtime.seed=0} in
    Phase~3 configs.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item[\checkmark] All attack hyperparameters listed in
    Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams_full} and the \texttt{configs/} YAML
    files.  No hyperparameter was selected post-hoc on the test set.

    intervals on binary metrics (Section~\ref{sec:phase3_results}).

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item[\checkmark] All experiments run on a single NVIDIA L4 GPU
    (Google Colab Pro).
  \item[\checkmark] Software: CUDA~12.x, PyTorch~2.x, Ultralytics~8.x,
    \texttt{lpips}~0.1.4, Python~3.10.
  \item[\checkmark] Detector fine-tuning and Phase~3 ablation are
    independent runs; no test-time data leakage.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
